Information Ontology (IO): Beyond the Tyranny of Mathematical Formalism

Rowan Brad Quni, QNFO

In the course of multi-year ongoing research, a shift from Information Dynamics to Information Ontology (IO) is more than a semantic adjustment—it is a philosophical and methodological realignment. The term “ontology” roots the framework in the study of what exists and how it exists, emphasizing the primacy of information as the fundamental substance of reality. This reframing also subtly encodes a duality: information is both dynamic (process) and ontological (substance). The acronym “IO” itself carries a quiet elegance, evoking binary input/output operations, the Greek goddess of mythic communication, and even the duality of inside/outside—a fitting metaphor for a framework that seeks to explain how internal informational relations give rise to external, observable reality.

The Misguided Worship of Mathematical Formalism

Conventional physics operates under an unspoken dogma: that the ultimate theory of reality must be expressible as a unified mathematical formalism—preferably a Lagrangian or Hamiltonian so pristine that it fits on a t-shirt. This belief persists despite Gödel’s devastating blow to the myth of complete formalization. Gödel himself, a mathematician, demonstrated that no sufficiently rich formal system can be both complete and consistent. And yet, the physics community continues to chase after ever-more-elaborate equations, as if complexity were a virtue rather than a warning sign.

Take, for example, the Langlands program—a sprawling, decades-long effort to unify number theory and geometry through labyrinthine mathematical structures. Its proposed “grand unified” descriptions often sprawl across hundreds of pages, requiring entire subfields of specialized notation just to parse. If nature’s deepest truths are indeed simple and universal, why should their formal description require such Byzantine machinery? The very fact that these models balloon into incomprehensibility suggests something is deeply wrong. A theory that reportedly takes 300 pages (not to mention an entire career) to state is not a theory of nature; it is a theory of itself—a self-referential artifact of mathematical aesthetics, not physical reality.

The Absurdity of Deterministic Lagrangian Obsession

The obsession with Lagrangians—equations that encode the dynamics of a system—reveals another flaw in the conventional paradigm: the assumption that reality must be deterministically describable at all levels. This is not just empirically dubious (given quantum indeterminacy), but logically incoherent. If Gödel tells us that even arithmetic contains truths unprovable within its own system, why should we expect a single equation to capture the entirety of a universe capable of generating mathematicians who ponder such equations?

The insistence on a “final Lagrangian” is a kind of intellectual cargo cult—a belief that if we just arrange the symbols correctly, the universe will obediently yield its secrets. But reality is under no obligation to conform to our preferred notation. The complexity of these formalisms is not evidence of their profundity, but of their distance from the underlying simplicity they claim to describe.

Logic as the Path to Parsimony and Prediction

If mathematics cannot fully formalize its own foundations, why should we expect it to fully formalize reality? The alternative is not to abandon rigor, but to embrace a deeper kind of rigor: logic in its most fundamental sense. Logic is not mere philosophical musing—it is the operational backbone of computation, the engine of scientific falsification, and the structure of coherent reasoning itself.

IO does not reject mathematics, but it subordinates it to a broader logical framework. Instead of seeking a single overarching equation, IO treats mathematical models as emergent tools—useful for describing regularities within specific domains, but not as the ultimate arbiter of truth. The resolution parameter (ε), for instance, may never reduce to a tidy equation, but its behavior can be rigorously constrained through logical principles governing how information transitions from potentiality to actuality.

The Next Phase: Simplicity Through Logical Coherence

The future of IO lies not in chasing ever-more-complex formalisms, but in refining its logical core. This means:

  1. Embracing Incompleteness–Accepting that no single formalism will capture all of IO’s implications, and instead focusing on coherence across multiple descriptive layers.
  2. Prioritizing Prediction Over Derivation–Using computational simulations and logical inference to test IO’s consequences, rather than demanding analytical solutions for every emergent phenomenon.
  3. Rejecting Unnecessary Complexity–Recognizing that if a model requires 300 pages of dense notation, it has likely strayed from physical insight into mathematical self-indulgence.

The beauty of IO is that it does not need to “solve” reality in the conventional sense. It needs only to provide the most coherent, empirically robust, and logically consistent framework for understanding how information structures existence. In doing so, it sidesteps the dead ends of formalism while retaining the precision that makes science possible.

Gödel’s lesson was not that mathematics is useless, but that it has limits. IO’s strength is that it begins where mathematics ends—not in defiance of logic, but in fulfillment of it.
