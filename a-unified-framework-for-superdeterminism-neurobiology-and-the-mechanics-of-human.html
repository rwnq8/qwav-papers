To understand this solution, we must first clearly define the fundamental concept of physical locality.

The concept of locality is the common-sense rule that an object can only be affected by things that physically touch it or interact with it at the speed of light. In relativistic physics, locality dictates that no information or causal influence can travel faster than the universal speed limit, denoted by the constant c. The universe enforces this speed limit by requiring all electromagnetic and gravitational forces to propagate through the spacetime manifold as waves, taking measurable time to cross physical distances. Preserving this local causality is absolutely essential for a deterministic universe, as faster-than-light communication would shatter the rigid clockwork of the cosmos. The phenomenon of quantum entanglement initially appeared to violate this sacred rule, causing massive consternation among classical physicists.

Quantum entanglement is the bizarre phenomenon where two particles become so deeply linked that measuring one instantly seems to determine the state of the other, regardless of the distance between them. Mathematically, entangled particles are described by a single, inseparable wave function, meaning the quantum state of the entire system cannot be factored into the individual states of its constituent parts.
