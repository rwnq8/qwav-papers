Computational Universe

A Unified Theory of Multi-Scale Computation

Author: Rowan Brad Quni-Gudzinas Affiliation: QNFO Contact: rowan.quni@qnfo.org ORCID: 0009-0002-4317-5604 ISNI: 0000 0005 2645 6062 DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.17432891 Publication Date: 2025-10-24 Version: 1.0

Abstract: This work establishes computation as an ontological primitive from which physical reality emerges. Spacetime, matter, and energy are derived from a self-computing causal network of distinctions (\(A \neq B\)) realized through spontaneous symmetry breaking. The bacterial flagellar motor is presented as a physical embodiment of these principles, a system that both computes and measures, thus instantiating observational entanglement. A formal framework built on homotopy type theory, cohesive \(\infty\)-topos theory, and synthetic differential geometry is used to model computational identity across scales. Quantum, biological, and cognitive domains are unified using computational gauge theory, sheaf cohomology, and renormalization group flow. The theory manages its own epistemological closure by distinguishing between Object, Model, and Meta-Computation, transforming epistemological paradoxes into generative constraints. This framework provides a foundation for quantum-bio-inspired computing, offers a new perspective on computational cosmology, and grounds a participatory ontology that dissolves the subject-object dualism.

Keywords: computation, multi-scale computation, homotopy type theory, cohesive topos, scale invariance, computational physics, strange loops, self-reference, biological computation, quantum computation

### Appendix A: Homotopy Type Theory Formalizations
Theorem A.1: Computational equivalence is constructively witnessed by cubical paths.
Proof: Working in cubical type theory with interval object \(\mathbb{I}\), for computational systems \(A, B: \text{Type}\), we define the function \(\text{comp\_equiv}\) that turns an equivalence \(e\) into a path:
\[ \begin{aligned} \text{comp\_equiv} : (A \simeq B) &\to \text{Path}(\text{Type}, A, B) := \\ \lambda e. \langle i \rangle \text{Glue } A [(i = 0) &\to (A, \text{idEquiv } A), (i = 1) \to (B, e)] \\ \end{aligned} \]
This function constructs a higher-dimensional cube whose boundary at \(i=0\) is system \(A\) and at \(i=1\) is system \(B\), with the equivalence \(e\) specifying how to relate them. For a quantum system \(Q\), a biological system \(B\), and a digital system \(D\) with proven equivalences \(e: Q \simeq B\) and \(f: B \simeq D\), the computational identity across all three scales is witnessed by the composite path:
\[ \text{comp\_path} : \text{Path}(\text{Type}, Q, D) := \text{transp} (\text{comp\_equiv } e) (\text{comp\_equiv } f) \]
This provides an explicit, constructive witness for the claim that all three systems, despite their different physical substrates, are performing the same computation. This formalizes the principle of scale equivalence within the theory. \(\square\)
### Appendix B: Cohesive \(\infty\)-topos Constructions
Theorem B.1: The \(\infty\)-topos of multi-scale computational processes is cohesive.
Proof: Let \(\mathcal{C}\) be the \(\infty\)-category whose objects are computational processes at all physical scales, 1-morphisms are computational transformations, and higher morphisms are equivalences between these transformations. We define the cohesive structure via the following modalities, which form an adjoint triple \(\int \dashv \flat \dashv \sharp\): - Shape Modality \(ʃ\): Encodes the coarse-grained, homotopical essence of a computation, forgetting its specific state representation. - Flat Modality \(\flat\): Encodes the underlying discrete computational states of a process, forgetting its continuous evolution. - Sharp Modality \(\sharp\): Encodes the continuous limit of a computational process, forgetting its discrete steps.
These modalities are verified to satisfy the cohesion axioms. For instance, the computational differential, a natural transformation \(d : \int X \to \flat X\), connects the global behavior to the discrete states, representing the infinitesimal computational transitions that drive the system’s evolution. The existence of this structure proves that the space of all computations is cohesive, meaning its discrete, continuous, and global aspects are internally consistent. \(\square\)
### Appendix C: Synthetic Differential Geometric Calculations
Theorem C.1: Computational state spaces carry natural prequantum circle bundles with metaplectic correction.
Proof: Let \((M, \omega)\) be the symplectic manifold representing the computational state space, where the 2-form \(\omega = d\theta\) is derived from the information 1-form \(\theta\). The prequantization condition, which requires that the integral of \(\omega\) over any closed 2-surface is an integer multiple of \(2\pi\), is satisfied because information in any physical computation is ultimately quantized.
\[ [\omega] \in H^2(M, 2\pi\mathbb{Z}) \]
This condition guarantees the existence of a prequantum circle bundle over \(M\). The metaplectic structure is then given by a lift of the structure group of the frame bundle from the symplectic group \(\text{Sp}(n)\) to its double cover, the metaplectic group \(\text{Mp}(n)\). This is formally represented by a lift of the classifying map:
\[ M \to B\text{Sp}(n) \to BU(1)_\nabla \]
This correction incorporates the Maslov index into the computational phase, which is essential for correctly calculating interference effects in quantum biological and quantum computing systems. \(\square\)
### Appendix D: Computational Gauge Theory Derivations
Theorem D.1: The Yang-Mills-Higgs functional describes optimal computational configurations.
Definition: The computational Yang-Mills-Higgs functional is defined as:
\[ \mathcal{S}[\nabla, \Phi] = \int_M (\|F_\nabla\|^2 + \|d_\nabla\Phi\|^2 + V(\Phi)) \, d\text{vol} \]
where \(F_\nabla\) is the curvature of the computational connection (representing scale-transition anomalies), \(\Phi\) is the computational Higgs field (representing the separation between scales), and \(V(\Phi)\) is a potential (e.g., a Mexican hat potential) that drives spontaneous symmetry breaking between computational regimes.
Equations of Motion: The principle of least action yields the equations of motion:
\[ d_\nabla^* F_\nabla = J(\Phi), \quad d_\nabla^*d_\nabla\Phi = -V'(\Phi) \]
These equations describe the optimal, physically realized computational configurations as those that minimize curvature and potential energy.
Theorem D.2: BRST cohomology eliminates unphysical computational states.
Construction: For a computational gauge algebra \(\mathfrak{g}\), we introduce ghost fields \(c\) and antighost fields \(b\). The BRST operator \(Q\), which is nilpotent (\(Q^2=0\)), acts on the fields as:
\[ \begin{aligned} Q\phi &= d_\nabla\phi + \{c, \phi\} \\ Qc &= -(1/2)[c, c] \\ Qb &= F_\nabla \end{aligned} \]
The physical, gauge-invariant computational states are precisely the elements of the zeroth cohomology group, \(H^0_{\text{BRST}}(C^*(M, \mathfrak{g}))\). Higher cohomology groups classify the anomalies. \(\square\)
### Appendix E: Sheaf Cohomology Computations
Theorem E.1: The derived moduli stack of computational processes classifies scale-transition anomalies.
Construction: The derived stack of computational processes is defined as a functor from the opposite category of differential graded affine schemes to the category of simplicial sets:
\[ \text{CompStack} : \text{dAff}^{\text{op}} \to \text{sSet} \]
\[ R \mapsto \text{Map}(\text{Spec } R, \mathfrak{M}_{\text{comp}}) \]
where \(\mathfrak{M}_{\text{comp}}\) is the derived moduli space parametrizing computational states, transition morphisms, and all higher coherence data. The tangent complex to this stack, \(T\mathfrak{M}_{\text{comp}}[-1]\), encodes the infinitesimal deformations of computational processes. The cohomology of this complex, \(H^i(T\mathfrak{M}_{\text{comp}})\), classifies the \(i\)-fold obstructions to integrating local computational processes into a globally consistent multi-scale computation. A non-vanishing \(H^1\) indicates a first-order obstruction, while higher groups indicate more complex consistency failures. \(\square\)
### Appendix F: Renormalization Group Analysis
Theorem F.1: Computational complexity flows according to the Callan-Symanzik equation.
Derivation: We define the computational \(\beta\)-function as the logarithmic derivative of the computational coupling constant \(g\) with respect to the energy scale \(\mu\):
\[ \beta(g) = \mu \frac{\partial g}{\partial \mu} \]
For a transition between a quantum and a classical computational regime, this function has fixed points at \(g=0\) (trivial computation) and \(g=g^*\) (a non-trivial fixed point corresponding to critical computation at the scale transition). The Callan-Symanzik equation describes how the \(n\)-point correlation functions \(G^{(n)}\) of the computational system scale with energy:
\[ \left[\mu \frac{\partial}{\partial \mu} + \beta(g) \frac{\partial}{\partial g} + \gamma\right]G^{(n)} = 0 \]
This equation demonstrates that computational complexity is not absolute but is a scale-dependent quantity that flows, with universal properties near critical points. \(\square\)
### Appendix G: Physical System Derivations
Theorem G.1: The quantum biological path integral includes a biological action quantum and topological phase terms.
Formulation: The amplitude for a quantum biological process to transition from an initial to a final state is given by the Feynman path integral over all possible computational histories:
\[ \langle \text{final}|\text{initial}\rangle = \int \mathcal{D}[\text{path}] \exp(i\mathcal{S}[\text{path}]/\hbar_{\text{bio}}) \]
where \(\hbar_{\text{bio}}\) is a characteristic biological action quantum, and the action \(\mathcal{S}[\text{path}]\) for a process like photosynthetic energy transfer includes a topological term involving the Berry connection \(A\):
\[ \mathcal{S}[\text{path}] = \int dt \left[ (1/2)m\dot{x}^2 - V(x) + A \cdot \dot{x} \right] \]
The Berry connection term encodes the geometric phases that accumulate in the chromophore networks, which are crucial for the high efficiency of these biological computations. The convergence of this path integral is ensured by the physical constraints of the biological system. \(\square\)
